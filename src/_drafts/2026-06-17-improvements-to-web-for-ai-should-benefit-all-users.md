Jason Grigsby zeroes in on something Safari’s team said in their opposition to the WebMCP API that cuts right to the heart of how we should be thinking about AI and the web:

> When a site’s actions are hard for an agent to use, that is a gap in the page’s own semantics, and the fix, in our opinion, is to close it in the platform’s shared layers (HTML and ARIA), where the user, assistive technology, and agents all benefit.

That’s the right call. The instinct to create a separate, richer semantic layer for AI agents—while users who rely on assistive technology keep getting the same old gaps—should be resisted loudly and often. If AI agents are, in effect, assistive technology (the Safari team makes this point directly), then treating their needs as separate from accessibility needs isn’t just architecturally messy; it’s a values error.

Jason pushes this further and suggests the Safari team’s framing ought to be canonized as a W3C Design Principle, which I’d fully support. Fix the platform. Fix the semantics. Everything else—agents, assistive tech, future surfaces we haven’t imagined yet—benefits as a consequence. That’s how good platform work is supposed to operate.
