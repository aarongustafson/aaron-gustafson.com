<p>In a recent post, <a href="http://blog.adrianroselli.com/2012/01/no-dhtml-please.html">Adrian Roselli ranted a bit about the awkward position we are in with regard to <span class="caps">HTML</span>5</a>. Here’s a taste:</p>
<blockquote cite="http://blog.adrianroselli.com/2012/01/no-dhtml-please.html">
<p>The trend continues where I speak to clients, vendors, young developers fresh out of college, and even the teachers/professors who instruct them and they don’t understand that <span class="caps">HTML</span>5 and <span class="caps">CSS</span>3 aren’t the same specification. I have repeatedly shown an <span class="caps">HTML</span> 4.01 site with <span class="caps">CSS</span>3 to explain that they are each distinct specifications which can be applied in different combinations of different versions. This is further complicated when JavaScript is folded into the mix—some folks even think jQuery is part of the <span class="caps">HTML</span>5 specification.</p>

<!-- more -->

</blockquote>
<p>It’s true: For all intents and purposes, “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5” has become a meaningless catch-all marketing phrase defining a platform rather than a specification. It’s “<span class="caps">DHTML</span>” all over again.<sup><a href="index.html#footnote-1">1</a></sup></p>
<p>This all probably started with the fact that “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5,” as a spec, was always more than a markup language. Even from the very early days at the <a href="http://whatwg.org"><span class="caps">WHATWG</span></a> (before they decided to go versionless and just call it “<span class="caps">HTML</span>”), “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5” was a markup language, an updated <span class="caps">DOM</span> interface, and a set of new <abbr title="Application Programming Interfaces">APIs</abbr> for interacting with browsers and devices. A few of us took issue with classifying it all as “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5,” but <a href="http://adactio.com/journal/4982/">it’s not like anyone can tell Hixie what to do</a>.</p>
<p>So yeah, from the beginning “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5” has been a bit of a misnomer, but the final blow to <span class="caps">HTML</span>5’s usefulness as a term—to me at least—came in the form of Apple’s “<a href="http://www.apple.com/html5/"><span class="caps">HTML</span>5 Showcase</a>.” It received a ton of attention in the press and really got the term “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5” out there… oddly enough while mostly demoing <span class="caps">CSS</span>3 features and making little to no attempt to disambiguate the technologies at work.</p>
<p>Ok, so what’s the problem with all of this? Some argue that there is no problem, that the public’s enthusiasm for “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5” can only bring about positive change on the web. I don’t disagree with that, but I also strongly believe semantics are important. Chris Mills summed up my feelings pretty well <a href="http://www.webstandards.org/2011/01/18/regarding-the-html5-logo/">on the <abbr title="Web Standards Project">WaSP</abbr> blog</a> around this time last year:</p>
<blockquote cite="http://www.webstandards.org/2011/01/18/regarding-the-html5-logo/">
<p>This really isn’t good—I appreciate that it is good to have an umbrella  term for a group of related technologies and techniques that would  otherwise be difficult to talk about in conversation. “Ajax” and “Web  2.0” serve that purpose well. And it is ok to talk about closely-related  specs such as <a href="http://dev.w3.org/geo/api/spec-source.html">Geolocation</a> and <a href="http://www.w3.org/TR/websockets/">Web Sockets</a> as being under the <span class="caps">HTML</span>5 umbrella, as long as you clarify it somewhere (you can find a good example in <a href="http://dev.opera.com/articles/view/get-familiar-with-html5/">Get familiar with <span class="caps">HTML</span>5!</a>).  But this is different—<span class="caps">HTML</span>5 and <span class="caps">CSS</span>3, for example, are two distinctly  different technologies, and should not be confused with one another. To  do so will impede learning and cause problems with development,  documentation, and all manner of other things.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>That’s the rub. When engaging in conversations, we need too know which “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5” is being discussed. Personally, when I discuss <span class="caps">HTML</span>5, I always draw a distinction between “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5” the marketing term and “<span class="caps">HTML</span>5” the specification even if asked ambiguous questions about <span class="caps">HTML</span>5. I’m not saying everyone needs to know the precise differences between the two uses of the term, but it’s our duty to educate them that there <em>is</em> a difference, even if they can’t fathom the particulars.</p>
<ol class="footnotes">
<li id="footnote-1"><span class="caps">DHTML</span>, as you may recall, was a catch-all phrase that meant using <span class="caps">HTML</span>, <span class="caps">CSS</span>, and JavaScript together, but some people thought it was an actual technology in its own right. Thanks marketing wonks!</li>
</ol>
