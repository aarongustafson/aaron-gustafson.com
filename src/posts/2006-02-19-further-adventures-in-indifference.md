<p>As opposed to just adding it to the comments in my original post, I decided to post the continuation of <a href="/notebook/apparently-some-people-just-dont-care/">my email conversation with the unnamed executive</a> at <a href="http://www.cronin-co.com">my former employer</a> about <a href="http://www.boxofchocolates.ca/archives/taking-aim-at-target-dot-com">the Target.com lawsuit</a> as a new entry. This is mostly <a href="/notebook/apparently-some-people-just-dont-care/#comment-268">for Derek’s amusement</a>, but I thought of a few other things to say on the subject as well.</p>

<!-- more -->

<p>We’ll start with his response. This is copied directly from his email; I take no credit for the spelling, grammar, etc.:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I understand all that you are saying.  The difference I see is a “public” building like a Target store is an impediment of it doesn’t have a ramp, etc. and it does matter. It is a physical, public places that discriminates if they don’t have the ramps, etc.</p>
<p>I just see the web as slightly different.  While ‘public’ in a sense, it is just as easy for a disabled person to find a web accessible site as it is to find a non-accessible one.  You don’t have to drive or walk anywhere.  Just click on a different URL. If Target discriminates against people with disabilities it is their problem, not mine.</p>
<p>So if Target doesn’t make their site accessible, they lose for all the reasons you state.  And why do I care if Target loses out on all the things you mention?  It is just as easy to click on walmart.com or wherever to find the accessibility you need.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>And my response to his:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I agree that it is not <em>your</em> problem as a citizen of the USA/world/universe/whatever. And, personally, I could care less if most major corporations blipped out of existence. But we (as marketers and people responsible for our clients’ online marketing/branding/presence/etc.) need to be aware of this and know that overcoming this “obstacle” does not take much <em>and</em> that the benefits far outweigh the time and money involved. We need to be able to work with our clients <strong>in their best interests</strong>, guiding them down the right path from a business standpoint, <strong>even if the benefits are not immediately apparent to them</strong>.</p>
<p>Now I’m not condoning the lawsuit, but it also helps from a PR standpoint not to get sued by a group of citizens with disabilities. After all, who’s going to look like the asshole there?</p>
</blockquote>
<p>After hitting send, I (of course) thought of a bunch of other stuff to say. I will spare you the entirety of my thoughts save this one:</p>
<p>It is not <q>just as easy for a disabled person to find a web accessible site as it is to find a non-accessible one.</q> There is a real dearth of e-commerce sites on the web that are accessible. <em>That</em> is a major part of the problem. Perhaps if more e-commerce software companies took a page from <a href="http://www.karova.com">Karova</a>, users surfing the web with disabilities or (shock) JavaScript turned off might be able to <em>choose</em> to shop somewhere other than a Target or a Wal-Mart or any other store which does not meet their accessibility (or <a href="http://www.boxofchocolates.ca/archives/accessibility-and-availability">availability</a>) requirements.</p>
<p>Anyway, he hasn’t responded to that last email and perhaps he never will. After all, <a href="/notebook/leap/">I gave my notice the next day</a>. And, no, this exchange was not responsible for that in any way.</p>
