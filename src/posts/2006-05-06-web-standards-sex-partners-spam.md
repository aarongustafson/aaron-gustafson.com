<p>I just got the best comment spam ever:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I’m very, very impressed that this sort of work is being done; Web Design is getting stagnant with people using just styled  block-level elements to produce artwork. The incorporation of <abbr title="Scalable Vector Graphics"><span class="caps">SVG</span></abbr> into sites excites me a lot.</p>

<!-- more -->

<p>How long do you expect it will take for this sort of technology to be widespread?</p>
<p>Obviously you can only speak about WebKit realistically, but if it’s going to take ten years for <abbr title="Internet Explorer"><span class="caps">IE</span></abbr> Win to gain (full) support,  we can’t design with it.</p>
<p>I’m amused by the “Becoming more important” line in the first paragraph. This has been a <span class="caps">HUGE</span> problem for years -  ever since <abbr title="HyperText Markup Language"><span class="caps">HTML</span></abbr>-2.0 was introduced to be more of a layout language and less of a markup language. For an example,  you just have to look at this site <a href="index.html#" rel="nofollow">sex partners</a> <em>[link removed -ed.]</em>. Why is all the text  crammed over on the left side of the page with a big blank space on the right side?</p>
<p>Why is the default font tiny and unreadable? Fortunately most browsers now let you override the latter problem.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>It is a little disjointed, but the fact that it mentions “block-level elements,” <abbr title="Scalable Vector Graphics"><span class="caps">SVG</span></abbr>, WebKit and (of course) “sex partners” is hysterical. Someone’s been paying attention in my training sessions.</p>
